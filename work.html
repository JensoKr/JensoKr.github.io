June 2026 · Browser-based tool

released — herbIQ, an interactive ecosystem estimator

Trained a machine‑learning regressor on coupled LPJ‑GUESS × Madingley simulation output: this hands‑on tool compress the multi‑trophic ecosystem response down to six sliders covering herbivore biomass, abundance and climate. Predictions for NPP, leaf biomass and herbivory pressure update in real time.

Built as a self‑contained desktop application for macOS and Windows first, then distilled into a single 3.3 MB ONNX package that runs entirely client‑side via onnxruntime‑web — no backend, no telemetry. The web preview lets curious readers play with the model directly in the browser; the native desktop build offers additional features.

December 2025 · Geoscientific Model Development

published — Full Coupling of LPJ-GUESS and Madingley

The culmination of years of model development: a first‑author paper in Geoscientific Model Development introducing the fully coupled LPJ‑GUESS × Madingley system. The work documents a multi‑platform interface that lets the dynamic vegetation model and the food‑web simulator exchange leaf biomass, herbivory pressure as fluxes — closing the loop between plant productivity and animal communities.

In simple terms: trees and grasses respond to the animals that eat them, and animals respond to the vegetation they live in — in the same simulation, at large scale. This opens the door to studying biodiversity feedbacks under climate change at scales that neither model could reach on its own.

April 2025 · Karlsruhe

Doctorate "dr. rer. nat." at KIT

I defended my dissertation — “Ecosystem Feedback from Animal‑Vegetation Interactions: A Modeling Approach” — before an expert committee at the Karlsruhe Institute of Technology. The thesis pulls together six years of work on coupled ecosystem modelling and reshapes how vegetation and animal communities can be simulated as a single dynamic system.

Awarded magna cum laude. A milestone I’ll be proud of for a long time, and the formal close to an intense, formative chapter that started in 2018 with the first attempts at making LPJ‑GUESS and Madingley talk to each other.

September 2023 · Leipzig

Poster — European Conference of Ecological Modelling 2023

Presented the in‑progress version of the fully coupled LPJ‑GUESS × Madingley model system at ECEM in Leipzig — the largest gathering of ecological modellers in Europe. The poster walked through the coupling interface, the first validation results against standalone runs and the open questions that the final version needs to solve.

This turned into two highly productive days: long conversations with competitive groups that are working on similar multi‑trophic couplings and sharp feedback that directly shaped the emerging GMD paper.

July 2023 · Garmisch‑Partenkirchen

Organised the Friends of Madingley Meeting

After attending the 2019 edition in Cambridge, I pushed the idea of a post‑pandemic relaunch of the Friends of Madingley meeting — a small, focused gathering of everyone developing or applying the Madingley model.

I drafted the multi‑day agenda, ran the logistics for the meeting at KIT’s Campus Alpin in Garmisch and co‑chaired the sessions together with Almut Arneth. Running it end‑to‑end taught me as much about scientific community‑building, time management and quiet quick-fixes as it did about the model itself.

Organisation Co‑chair Community building KIT Campus Alpin
September 2022 · Ecological Modelling

published — One-Way Coupling of LPJ‑GUESS and Madingley

Published in Ecological Modelling (Issue 471), this first‑author paper presents the one‑way coupling: how the dynamic global vegetation simulated by LPJ‑GUESS can be used as inputs for Madingley to run its food‑web simulation — net primary production, leaf‑mass distributions, plant functional types and fire dynamics, all distributed across diverse ecosystem types.

The article documents the coupling interface, validates the coupled outputs against the standalone Madingley defaults and discusses the limitations that motivated the later full coupling. This is a solid foundation to build upon.

February 2020 · Davos

Poster Session at the World Biodiversity Forum

My first major international conference — and what an introduction it was. I presented an early version of the LPJ‑GUESS × Madingley coupling at the inaugural World Biodiversity Forum in Davos, an interdisciplinary initiative hosted at the same venue as the World Economic Forum.

Sharing a room with researchers whose papers I had been reading for years was both intimidating and inspiring. During the poster session, my assumptions about the model were critically challenged, which broadened my perspective on potential real‑world applications. The panel discussions that followed were equally memorable: hearing leading scientists reflect on their work, and on how political decisions shape both research and academic careers, left a lasting impression on me.

Poster Presentation WBF 2020 Davos First major conference
Mai 2018 · Hanover

Completed my M.Sc. in Theoretical Meteorology

Earned my Master of Science in Meteorology at Leibniz Universität Hannover. After developing a digital weather‑forecast information panel as my Bachelor’s project, the Master’s thesis took me into the numerical end of meteorology: Large‑Eddy Simulations of surface heterogeneities — modelling how patchy land surfaces (forests, urban areas, mixed fields) shape turbulence in the lowest layers of the atmosphere during the LITFASS-experiments.

The skills that mattered most — reading complex numerical codes, running high‑resolution simulations, and quietly debugging them — turned out to transfer almost one‑to‑one into my later work.
